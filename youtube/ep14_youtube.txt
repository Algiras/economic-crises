On September 18, 1873, Jay Cooke and Company — the most prestigious banking house in the United States, financier of the Union during the Civil War — declared bankruptcy. Within hours the New York Stock Exchange closed for ten days. Eighteen thousand businesses failed over the next two years. The Long Depression had begun.

What followed was not a sharp crash with a clear recovery but something more insidious: two decades of continuous deflation, falling prices, and stagnating wages that eroded debts for creditors while destroying livelihoods for everyone who owed money. There were no macroeconomic tools to respond — no central bank, no fiscal policy as we understand it, no mechanism to expand demand — and so the gold standard's deflationary logic simply ran its course. The Long Depression ended not through intervention but through structural transformation: industrialisation, monetary reform, and the political pressures that would culminate in the progressive era.
