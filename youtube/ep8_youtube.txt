On Monday, October 19, 1987, the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell five hundred and eight points — twenty-two and a half percent in a single day. It remains the largest single-day percentage decline in stock market history.

Black Monday exposed three structural failures simultaneously: the illusion of liquidity created by portfolio insurance strategies that assumed someone would always be on the other side of a trade, the amplifying effect of computerised program trading, and the absence of any mechanism to interrupt self-reinforcing selling. The Federal Reserve's one-sentence statement the following morning — that it stood ready as a lender of last resort to support the financial system — was sufficient to stop the panic, and the feared recession never came. What changed permanently was the architecture of markets: circuit breakers, coordinated margin rules, and direct Fed communication with exchange specialists all date to October 1987.
