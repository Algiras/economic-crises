On December 1, 2001, Argentina's government froze bank deposits. Argentines called it the corralito — the little corral. Citizens could not access their own savings. Within weeks the country had five presidents in two weeks, the largest sovereign default in history, and an economy in freefall.

Argentina's crisis illustrated the impossible trinity in its most brutal form: a currency board pegged to the dollar, free capital mobility, and an independent monetary policy could not coexist — and when the peg broke, everything broke at once. The solution was unilateral and radical: Argentina defaulted on $100 billion in sovereign debt, abandoned convertibility, and forcibly converted dollar-denominated contracts into pesos at rates that wiped out creditors. The recovery was swift and surprising — eight percent annual growth from 2003 to 2007 — and the episode permanently altered how economists think about currency boards, IMF conditionality, and the politics of sovereign default.
