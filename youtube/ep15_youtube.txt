In October 2009, Greece's newly elected government announced that its predecessor had been misreporting the public deficit. The figure was not three point seven percent of GDP. It was twelve point seven. Later revisions put it at fifteen point four. Greece had been lying to the European Union about its finances for years — and the EU had been accepting the numbers without auditing them.

Greece's crisis exposed a structural design flaw built into the euro from its creation: a monetary union without a fiscal union, where member states shared a currency but not a budget, a lender of last resort, or a mechanism for rebalancing. The initial response — austerity-conditioned bailouts from the IMF and ECB — deepened the recession it was meant to contain, and it took Mario Draghi's three-word commitment in July 2012 to do "whatever it takes" to finally arrest contagion. What changed permanently was the architecture of the eurozone itself: a banking union, the ESM, and a central bank willing to act as a genuine backstop.
