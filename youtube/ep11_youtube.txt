In 1993, John Meriwether assembled the most credentialed team in financial history — two future Nobel laureates, a former Federal Reserve vice chairman, and the architects of modern derivatives pricing. Long-Term Capital Management was not merely a hedge fund. It was a test of whether financial theory could generate risk-free profits from mathematical certainty. Between 1994 and 1997, it returned forty percent annually. The fund's models said its probability of losing everything was one in ten to the twenty-fourth power — impossible in any timescale relevant to human civilisation.

In August 1998, Russia defaulted on its domestic debt. LTCM had minimal Russian exposure. It didn't matter. In a panic, assets that normally move independently move together — and LTCM's supposedly uncorrelated positions all moved against it simultaneously because every bank on Wall Street had copied its trades. A fund with $125 billion in assets and over $1 trillion in notional derivatives lost ninety percent of its capital in five weeks. On September 23, 1998, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York gathered fourteen banks in a room and told them: coordinate a bailout or watch each other fail. The crisis passed. The lesson — that mathematical sophistication is not risk management — was not applied. The 2008 crisis would demonstrate the cost.
