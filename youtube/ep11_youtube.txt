In 1993, John Meriwether assembled the most credentialed team in financial history. Two of his partners would win the Nobel Prize in economics. The fund returned forty percent annually for four years. Then, in five weeks in 1998, it nearly brought down the global financial system.

LTCM's failure was a category error: its models measured risk as volatility, but real risk is correlated failure — when every position moves against you at once because everyone holds the same positions. When Russia defaulted in August 1998, every correlation LTCM's models said was impossible went to one simultaneously, and a fund with $125 billion in assets and over $1 trillion in notional derivatives faced forced liquidation. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York organised a private bailout of fourteen banks on September 23, 1998 — establishing the too-big-to-fail template that would define the next decade of crisis management.
