On December 29, 1989, the Nikkei 225 closed at thirty-eight thousand nine hundred and fifteen — its all-time high. By 1992 it had fallen sixty percent. Japanese real estate followed. The country that had looked like the future of capitalism lost an entire decade.

Japan's lost decade was a paradox of thrift operating at institutional scale: every rational actor — households, businesses, banks — simultaneously tried to repair its balance sheet, and the combined effect was a deflationary trap that no single actor could escape. The government launched seventeen fiscal stimulus packages and the Bank of Japan cut rates to zero, yet nominal GDP in 2003 was roughly what it had been in 1992. The episode became the defining template for understanding liquidity traps, zombie banks, and why balance-sheet recessions do not respond to conventional monetary policy.
