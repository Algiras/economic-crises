On the morning of October 22, 1907, the Knickerbocker Trust Company of New York suspended payments to depositors. Within hours, lines stretched around the block. Within days, the panic had spread to a dozen institutions. There was no central bank to stop it.

The Panic of 1907 exposed the structural gap Walter Bagehot had described forty years earlier: without a lender of last resort, any liquidity crisis can become a solvency crisis through contagion alone. J.P. Morgan — a private citizen — convened bank presidents in his library, locked the doors, and personally organised the liquidity injections that stopped the panic, functioning as an improvised central bank for a country of seventy million people. The episode was sufficiently alarming that it triggered six years of political effort culminating in the Federal Reserve Act of 1913.
