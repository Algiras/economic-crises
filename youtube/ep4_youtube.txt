In November 1923, a loaf of bread in Berlin cost two hundred billion marks. A month earlier it had cost five billion. A year earlier it had cost one hundred and sixty.

Weimar Germany's hyperinflation was not an accident of incompetence — it was the terminal consequence of a war financed by debt, a punitive reparations settlement, and a monetary system with no remaining anchor. When France occupied the Ruhr in January 1923 and Germany funded passive resistance by printing money, the mark went from thousands-to-the-dollar to trillions-to-the-dollar in months. The cure — the Rentenmark introduced on November 15, 1923 — worked not because it was backed by gold but because it was backed by a credible commitment to stop printing.
