On September 15, 2008, at one forty-five in the morning, Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy. Within hours, the Reserve Primary Fund — one of the oldest money market funds in America — announced it had broken the buck. Money market funds were supposed to be as safe as cash. The crisis that had been visible in slow motion for eighteen months became systemic in a single morning.

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis was not primarily a crisis of household mortgages. It was a crisis of the financial architecture built on top of those mortgages — a twelve-trillion-dollar shadow banking system operating with almost no regulatory oversight and no lender of last resort. The originate-to-distribute model had separated mortgage lending from mortgage risk; credit default swaps had concentrated rather than dispersed that risk; and the tools available to the Federal Reserve and Treasury had been designed for a transparent system of regulated banks, not the one that actually existed. On October 13, 2008, nine bank chief executives were summoned to the Treasury, presented with capital injection term sheets, told the amounts were not negotiable, and all nine signed within an hour. Zero senior bankers were subsequently prosecuted. Ninety-one percent of income gains between 2009 and 2012 went to the top one percent. The political consequences lasted a decade.
