On March 10, 2000, the NASDAQ Composite Index peaked at 5,048 — five times its level four years earlier. Within eighteen months it fell seventy-eight percent. Five trillion dollars in market value evaporated.

The dot-com crash was not simply a valuation correction — it exposed conflicts of interest that had been visible but tolerated throughout the boom: investment banks underwriting IPOs while analysts publicly rated the same stocks as buys, venture capitalists engineering liquidity events before business models were proven, and a financial press that functioned more as a promotional apparatus than as a check. The Federal Reserve's response — aggressive rate cuts after the September 11th attacks — prevented a deep recession but planted the conditions for the next speculative cycle. What changed permanently were the rules governing analyst independence, IPO allocation, and the separation of research from investment banking.
