On August 12, 1982, Mexico's finance minister telephoned the United States Treasury and the IMF. Mexico could not make its debt payments. The announcement that followed triggered a decade of crisis across an entire continent.

Petrodollars recycled through Western banks had been lent to Latin American governments at variable interest rates, and when the Volcker shock sent rates to twenty percent, debt service costs doubled on loans underwritten on the assumption rates would stay low. Three failures compounded each other: loans that should never have been made at variable rates to sovereigns, a monitoring failure by lenders who assumed sovereign borrowers could not default, and IMF conditionality that demanded austerity precisely when economies needed expansion. The Brady Plan of 1989 — debt-for-equity swaps and bond exchanges — finally resolved the crisis, but only after a lost decade of roughly eight percent per capita income contraction across the region.
