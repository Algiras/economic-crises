In January 2022, Sharon Kowalski, a retired Pittsburgh school administrator on a fixed pension of twenty-eight hundred dollars a month, watched her weekly grocery bill rise from eighty-seven to one-hundred-and-twenty-four dollars. Eggs had gone from a dollar thirty-nine to three dollars and eighty-nine cents. She had not had a pay rise in three years. She would not get one.

The Great Inflation of 2021–2023 was not a single phenomenon — it was three or four overlapping inflation processes operating simultaneously: excess pandemic savings deployed into a supply-constrained reopening economy, energy prices weaponised by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and a Federal Reserve that had held rates at zero while dismissing inflation as transitory until CPI reached nine-point-one percent. The correction was equally forceful: eleven rate hikes, four consecutive seventy-five basis point increases — the most aggressive tightening since Volcker. On August 26, 2022, Jerome Powell's thirteen-hundred-word Jackson Hole speech wiped one-point-three trillion dollars from the S&P 500 in an afternoon. Inflation fell from nine-point-one to two-point-four percent without the recession that every previous serious tightening cycle had produced. The soft landing Volcker never achieved appeared to have occurred — at a cost distributed, as always, with characteristic unevenness.
