In October 1973, OPEC announced an oil embargo against the United States. The price of oil quadrupled in months. Within a year, inflation hit twelve percent — while unemployment simultaneously rose above nine percent. Economists had a word for this combination. They borrowed it because no existing model could explain it.

The 1970s stagflation crisis involved four simultaneous structural failures, and each attempted solution made at least one of the others worse: Vietnam War spending and the breakdown of Bretton Woods had already inflated the money supply before the oil shock arrived; Nixon's wage and price controls deferred inflation rather than curing it; the Federal Reserve accommodated rising prices for fear of causing recession; and once workers and businesses expected prices to rise, their behaviour made prices rise. The resolution came on Saturday October 6, 1979, when Paul Volcker called an emergency Federal Open Market Committee meeting timed between the US market close and the Asian open — and announced he would cause a recession if that was what breaking inflation required. It was. Unemployment hit ten-point-eight percent. The Volcker recession permanently destroyed two-point-two million manufacturing jobs. The lesson absorbed: inflation targeting works when central banks are willing to impose genuine pain. The lesson not absorbed: the cost of that pain falls almost entirely on workers and debtors.
