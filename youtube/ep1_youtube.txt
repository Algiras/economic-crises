On the morning of October 29, 1929, Charles Mitchell, chairman of National City Bank, watched the ticker tape fall two hours behind. The numbers scrolling past represented the accumulated savings of millions of Americans being destroyed in real time. The crash was not the cause of the Great Depression. It was the signal flare that revealed what had already gone wrong.

The Great Depression exposed four interlocking failures that no single policy lever could address simultaneously: a banking system collapse that destroyed nine thousand banks and seven billion dollars in deposits, a deflationary debt trap that made every dollar owed worth more as incomes fell, a gold standard that transmitted the crisis globally and prevented monetary expansion, and a policy response that raised taxes and tightened budgets at exactly the wrong moment. The New Deal — deposit insurance, the SEC, dollar devaluation, direct relief — stopped total collapse but did not end the Depression. That required the spending of the Second World War. The lesson that was absorbed: banking systems need oversight. The lesson that was forgotten: two years of inaction turned a financial crisis into a civilisational trauma.
