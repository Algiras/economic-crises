The Thai baht collapsed. Within months, currencies across East Asia were in freefall. How did one small country trigger a regional catastrophe?

The 1997–1998 Asian financial crisis was a crisis of capital flows — rapid liberalisation without adequate regulatory infrastructure left emerging economies exposed to sudden reversals. It reshaped how the IMF operates and how developing nations manage their foreign reserves. The lessons were partly learned. The structural vulnerabilities that enabled it have reappeared elsewhere.
