In the summer of 1720, shares of the South Sea Company — a British joint-stock corporation holding a monopoly on trade with Spanish South America — rose from one hundred pounds to one thousand pounds in eight months. Then they fell back to one hundred pounds in eight weeks. Thousands of investors, including members of Parliament and the aristocracy, were ruined.

The South Sea Bubble was not simply a case of irrational exuberance: it was a deliberate scheme built on bribery, insider information, and the conversion of government debt into equity at fraudulent valuations, enabled by the same politicians who were supposed to regulate it. The immediate response — the Bubble Act of 1720, which prohibited unauthorised joint-stock companies — suppressed the instrument that caused the bubble but stunted British corporate finance for over a century. What the episode established permanently was the concept of the speculative mania itself: the archetype against which every subsequent bubble has been measured.
